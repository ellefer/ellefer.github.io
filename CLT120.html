We're going to demonstrate the Central Limit Theorem in R with some simulations.

Let's start with flipping coins (this is a Bernoulli Distribution). We get each student in a class of 30 to flip a coin 25 times. (This is 30âˆ—25=75030*25 = 75030âˆ—25=750 coin flips). We would expect out of all these coin flips, about 750/2=375750/2 = 375750/2=375 of them would be heads and 375375375 would be tails.

In the cell below, we simulate this. rbinom(n,1,.5) represents a student flipping a coin n times with probability of heads equal to 1/2. replicate(m,rbinom(n,1,.5)) repeats this for m students. We plot a histogram of the samples, and see it is quite close to half heads and half tails.

You can play with the number of flips per student, n, and the number of students m, (total number of flips nâˆ—mn*mnâˆ—m) to see how this gets generally gets closer to 50\50 as the number of flips increases.

This looks like samples from a normal distribution! You can go back and increase nnn to convince yourself that as our samples (number of flips) get larger, we get closer to normal. You can also increase mmm to get more data from the distribution.

Remember, these are the same data points we used in the first plot that looked uniform. The difference between the two plots being: in the first, we plotted each individual sample, and in the second, we took the sum of sample sizes nnn (sum of total number of Heads) and then plotted those sums. The Central Limit Theorem tells us that the sum of a distribution will approach a normal distribution as the sample from the distribution, nnn, gets larger.

Another cool thing: we don't care about the distribution! Let's do this with another distribution to demonstrate. Let's start with a sample from XXX ~ exp(1)exp(1)exp(1) (An exponential distribution with rate parameter (mmm) equal to 1). n will be our sample size. The command rexp(n,1) gives us n samples from a exp(1)exp(1)exp(1) distribution, and geom_histogram plots the frequency of our samples. We are using a binwidth of .2.2.2 and starting our bins at 000.

Below, you can play with the sample size, n. As n increases, the histogram looks more and more like an exponential distribution. 

n = 100

Now, what happens when we take m samples of size n from an exponential distribution (i.e. we have m different plots of our samples similar to the one above). For each of those samples, we take the mean. Then, we have m means of samples from the exponential distribution. The samples themselves will look exponential, but the means will take on an (approximately) normal distribution (CLT).

The command mean(rexp(n,1)) takes a sample of size nnn from an exponential distribution with rate parameter 1, and then it takes the mean of that sample. replicate(m,mean(runif(n,0,1))) repeats this m times, so we have m means of samples of size n. We can plot these m means to see the distribution (below). It looks nothing like an exponential distribuiton... it looks normal! You can change the sample size n to see how it looks more and more like a normal distribution as n increases. You can also increase m to get a more accurate picture of the distribution. You might need to change the binwidth as you change mmm and nnn as well.

n = 50
m = 100
expMeans <- replicate(m,mean(rexp(n,1)))
ggplot(data.frame(unifMeans), aes(x = unifMeans)) + geom_histogram(binwidth = .001)

It says the sums of your samples or the means of your samples are normally distributed as long as the sample size, (nnn) is large enough!
